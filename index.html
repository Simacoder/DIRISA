Core knowledge

What is statistics, really?

Statistics is the science of learning from data under uncertainty. Spiegelhalter calls it "the art of making sense of numbers." Click each topic to explore.

Core definition
What is Statistics?
The science of collecting, analysing, interpreting, presenting, and organising data. It is the primary tool for making informed decisions under uncertainty — the condition in which all real-world decisions are made.
Population
Population vs. Sample
Population: every object about which information is sought. Sample: the subset actually observed. Most data science works with samples — never populations.
Spiegelhalter's insight
Metaphorical Population
Even when you appear to have all the data, treat it as a sample from an imaginary space of all possible outcomes that could have arisen. Every finding is an estimate — never a certainty.
Key distinction
Data ≠ Reality
A dataset is only a representation of reality. What is missing? Motivation, stress, family circumstances, language — variables that matter but go unmeasured. Always ask: what is not in this data?
Two branches
Descriptive vs. Inferential
Descriptive: summarise what you observe. Inferential: draw conclusions about what you did not observe. Both are necessary; neither is sufficient alone.
Core warning
Correlation ≠ Causation
Two variables can move together without one causing the other. Ice cream sales and drowning both rise in summer — because heat causes both. Always demand a logical mechanism before claiming causation.
The question before the data
Problem → Plan → Data → Analysis → Conclusion

Central tendency
The Mean (Average)
The sum of all values divided by n. Sensitive to outliers — one extreme value distorts it. Always report the mean alongside a measure of spread.
Robust centre
The Median
The middle value when data is ordered. Resistant to outliers. Preferred for income, house prices, and any skewed distribution where the mean would mislead.
Spread
Variance & Standard Deviation
Variance measures average squared distance from the mean. Standard deviation (σ) is its square root — the typical distance from the mean. Variability often matters more than the average.
Key formulas
Mean: x̄ = Σxᵢ / n
Variance: σ² = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / n
Std Dev: σ = √σ²
// Never report the mean without the standard deviation
Live example — student exam scores
EDA principle
Visualise Before Modelling
Always visualise the data before fitting a model. Charts reveal patterns, clusters, outliers, and errors that formulas cannot. A scatter plot takes 30 seconds; a wrong model takes weeks to diagnose.
Distribution shape
Normal vs. Skewed
Normal (Gaussian): bell curve, mean = median = mode. Right-skewed: tail to the right, mean > median. Left-skewed: tail to the left, mean < median.
Relationship
Pearson Correlation (r)
r ranges from −1 to +1. r = +1: perfect positive linear relationship. r = 0: no linear relationship. r = −1: perfect negative. Reminder: r measures association, not causation.
Pearson's r
r = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ) / [√Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² · √Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)²]
// r close to 1.0 does not mean causation — especially with small samples

Foundation
Probability
The language of uncertainty. P(A) = number of favourable outcomes / total possible outcomes. Ranges from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain). Everything in data science uses probability at its core.
Conditional
P(A|B) — Conditional Probability
The probability of A given B has occurred. P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B). Knowing something changes the odds. Foundation of recommendation systems and fraud detection.
Bayesian
Bayes' Theorem
P(H|E) = P(E|H) · P(H) / P(E). Update your belief (prior) using evidence (likelihood) to get a revised belief (posterior). The core mechanism of rational learning.
Bayes' theorem — the three steps
Prior P(H) → Likelihood P(E|H) → Posterior P(H|E)
P(H|E) = P(E|H) × P(H) / P(E)

Core concept
Hypothesis Testing
Start with a null hypothesis H₀ (nothing is happening). Collect data. Ask: how likely is this data if H₀ were true? If very unlikely, we reject H₀. The p-value quantifies this likelihood.
Spiegelhalter's warning
The p-value Problem
p < 0.05 does NOT mean the result is true. It means the data would be unlikely if H₀ were true. Many published significant results are false positives. Small samples amplify this problem enormously.
Precision
Confidence Intervals
A 95% CI means: if we repeated the study 100 times, roughly 95 of the intervals would contain the true population value. Not "95% probability the true value is in this interval."
Confidence interval for a mean
CI = x̄ ± z × (σ / √n)
where z = 1.96 for 95% confidence, σ = population std dev, n = sample size
// Larger n → narrower interval → more precise estimate

Spiegelhalter's framework

The PPDAC Cycle

Every rigorous statistical investigation follows five stages — in order. Skipping stages is how analysts reach wrong or misleading conclusions. Most students jump directly to Analysis.

Problem
State the question precisely
A vague question produces a useless answer. Before touching data, name: (1) the population of interest, (2) the outcome variable, (3) the candidate explanatory variables, (4) the decision the analysis should support. Vague: "predict marks." Precise: "which measurable factors among attendance, study hours, and assignment scores are most strongly associated with final exam performance for Level 1 students at this institution?"

The Shipman Warning: When the same statistical test was applied to all GPs, it surfaced a second outlier — with an entirely innocent explanation (elderly rural patients). Without domain context, statistical conclusions can be catastrophically wrong. The Problem stage must always come first.
